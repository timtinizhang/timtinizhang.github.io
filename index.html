Hi! This is Tim, and my full name is pronounced [Tin-ee Jahng].

I am a fourth-year undergraduate student majoring in International and Public Affairs at the University of Macau, where I am honored to be supported by the Golden Lotus Full Scholarship. From 2025 to 2027, I also serve as the Head of Academics of the Youth Association of International Affairs.

My research is situated in applied microeconomics, with particular interests in Political Economy, Labor Economics, and Development Economics. Specifically, I ask how production structures and distributional outcomes are shaped by forces such as the open economy, domestic institutions, and development strategies in China and East Asia, and what socioeconomic consequences have emerged during these economic transformations, especially in relation to broader issues and tensions in labor-market dynamics, human capital, information and belief formation, inequality, and social mobility, as well as the role of government policy.

My academic background combines substantive training in political economy with quantitative empirical methods. Through extensive coursework and hands-on experience, I have been equipped with solid skills in research design, causal inference, R programming, computational text analysis, survey experiments, and big data processing.

Research

Selected Papers in Progress

  1. Tracking Bill Changes in China's Two-phase Lawmaking Process: A Computational Text Analysis Approach (with Jiying Jiang)
    [Abstract]

    Why do some bills bypass the public consultation stage? How do legal provisions change under the two-phase legislative procedure? What roles do different issue areas and stakeholders play in this process? In this co-authored, ongoing project, we construct a dataset covering 486 laws promulgated by the 11th through 14th National People's Congresses of China, 381 public consultation records, and the draft texts at each stage of the two-phase legislative process for 98 of these laws, drawing on an efficient, AI-assisted data workflow that I develop. Using automated text analysis in R, we currently employ cosine similarity, Jaro–Winkler string distance, and related methods to match provisions and trace textual changes at the paragraph (kuan) level.

  2. Foreign Anti-Elite Narratives and Distributional Beliefs
    [Abstract]

    Existing research shows that information about foreign societies can reshape evaluations of domestic conditions through cross-country comparison and prior-belief updating. Yet it rarely distinguishes information about how well a foreign society performs from the causal narratives used to explain that performance. This ongoing project examines whether foreign anti-elite narratives travel across contexts and alter distributional beliefs in China. Using a survey experiment, it compares matched accounts of economic hardship abroad that either describe the hardship neutrally or attribute it to elite capture, labor-market unfairness, and blocked mobility. The study measures beliefs about domestic inequality, economic fairness, and upward mobility, and tests for heterogeneity by pretreatment economic insecurity and prior beliefs. By separating relative-performance signals from causal framing, the project distinguishes two potentially opposing channels—favorable cross-country benchmarking and cross-context narrative transfer—and contributes to empirical political economy and research on information, narratives, and distributional beliefs.

  3. Foreign Praise, Consumer Convenience, and Labor Protection in China
    [Abstract]

    This ongoing project examines how foreign praise of China's low-cost, high-speed services shapes urban consumers' views of the workers who provide them. It asks whether external validation makes convenience appear as a developmental achievement worth preserving, or instead draws attention to the labor conditions that sustain it. Using a survey experiment focused on app-based delivery, the project separates the effect of foreign praise from information about wages, working hours, and social protection. Primary outcomes include beliefs about worker conditions and choices among service bundles that trade off price and delivery time against worker pay and protection. By estimating how narratives alter consumers' willingness to bear the costs of stronger labor standards, the project links cross-border information flows to labor regulation, ethical consumption, and the distributional consequences of service-sector development in China.

About My Name

A kind note on my name: the two words "Tin" and "I" together make up my full legal first name. This means that "I" is not a middle name and should never be omitted in any formal context.

This mildly frustrating yet rather interesting quirk arises from the interplay of Chinese naming conventions, Cantonese romanization, and Macau law. In everyday life, however, please feel free to simply call me Tim—that is what most people call me.
