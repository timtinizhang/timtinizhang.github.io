Research

Research Interests

My primary research interest asks how economic openness, domestic institutions, and development strategies interact to reshape production structures and distributional outcomes in China and East Asia, and how these transformations further generate socioeconomic tensions across periods of growth and slowdown. I am especially interested in their consequences for beliefs and attitudes, labor-market dynamics, education choices, and social mobility, as well as how information flows and media narratives shape the ways individuals interpret and respond to these pressures.

A secondary line of my research analyzes how central and local officials navigate complex institutional and political-economic constraints to formulate economic policies and make political choices. This is particularly connected to issues of urbanization, infrastructure, and regional development.

Selected Papers in Progress

  1. Tracking Bill Changes in China's Two-phase Lawmaking Process: A Computational Text Analysis Approach (with Jiying Jiang)
    [Abstract]

    Why do some bills bypass the public consultation stage? How do legal provisions change under the two-phase legislative procedure? What roles do different issue areas and stakeholders play in this process? In this co-authored, ongoing project, we construct a dataset covering 486 laws promulgated by the 11th through 14th National People's Congresses of China, 381 public consultation records, and the draft texts at each stage of the two-phase legislative process for 98 of these laws, drawing on an efficient, AI-assisted data workflow that I develop. Using automated text analysis in R, we currently employ cosine similarity, Jaro–Winkler string distance, and related methods to match provisions and trace textual changes at the paragraph (kuan) level.

  2. Foreign Anti-Elite Narratives and Economic Beliefs: Evidence from China
    [Abstract]

    This ongoing project examines how foreign anti-elite narratives shape economic beliefs in China. Existing research suggests that negative information about foreign societies can improve evaluations of domestic conditions through favorable cross-country comparison. Using a survey experiment, the project tests whether narratives about inequality, labor-market hardship, and blocked mobility abroad instead make analogous domestic problems more salient. It measures beliefs about domestic inequality, labor-market fairness, and upward mobility, and tests for heterogeneity by economic insecurity. The central hypothesis is that economically secure respondents may use foreign hardship as a comparative benchmark, while economically insecure respondents may map the same distributive claims onto their own experience. By distinguishing anti-elite framing from foreign negative information more generally, the project examines how narrative content and material circumstances shape cross-border belief formation, contributing to the economics of information and political economy.

  3. Foreign Praise, Consumer Convenience, and Labor Protection: Evidence from China
    [Abstract]

    This ongoing project examines how foreign praise of China’s low-cost, high-speed services shapes urban consumers’ views of the workers who provide them. It asks whether external validation makes convenience appear as a developmental achievement worth preserving, or instead draws attention to the labor conditions that sustain it. Using a survey experiment focused on app-based delivery, the project separates the effect of foreign praise from information about wages, working hours, and social protection. Primary outcomes include beliefs about worker conditions and choices among service bundles that trade off price and delivery time against worker pay and protection. By estimating how narratives alter consumers’ willingness to bear the costs of stronger labor standards, the project links cross-border information flows to labor regulation, ethical consumption, and the distributional consequences of service-sector development in China.
